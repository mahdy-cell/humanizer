Digital Surveillance, Privacy Rights, and the Governance of Social Media Platforms: A Critical Analysis of Legal Frameworks in Post-GDPR Europe

Abstract

This paper presents a comprehensive critical analysis of the legal and socio-political frameworks governing digital surveillance and data privacy in post-GDPR Europe. Furthermore, it examines the transformative impact of the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) on the relationship between technology corporations, nation-states, and individual citizens. Drawing on critical theory, legal analysis, and empirical data from regulatory enforcement actions, this study argues that while the GDPR represents a significant step forward in protecting citizens' digital rights, its implementation has been characterised by inconsistent enforcement, jurisdictional ambiguities, and persistent power asymmetries between platform operators and data subjects.

1. Introduction

The proliferation of digital technologies has fundamentally transformed the landscape of social interaction, political participation, and economic exchange. Moreover, the comprehensive collection and analysis of personal data by technology corporations and state actors has given rise to what Shoshana Zuboff (2019) has termed "surveillance capitalism" — "the unilateral claiming of private human experience as free raw material for translation into behavioural data." At the end of the day, the tension between the imperatives of digital innovation and the protection of fundamental rights represents one of the defining governance challenges of the contemporary era.

The European Union's General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), which entered into force on 25 May 2018, constitutes the most comprehensive legislative attempt to regulate data processing activities and protect individuals' privacy rights. According to Article 5(1) of the GDPR:

"Personal data shall be: (a) processed lawfully, fairly and in a transparent manner in relation to the data subject ('lawfulness, fairness and transparency'); (b) collected for specified, explicit and legitimate purposes and not further processed in a manner that is incompatible with those purposes."

Furthermore, the regulation establishes a comprehensive framework of data subject rights, including the right to access, rectification, erasure, and data portability. Moreover, the GDPR imposes significant obligations on data controllers and processors, including the requirement to conduct data protection impact assessments (DPIAs) for processing activities that are likely to result in high risks to individuals' rights and freedoms.

2. Theoretical Framework

The analysis presented in this paper is informed by three interconnected theoretical perspectives:

• Critical Legal Studies (CLS): This approach examines how legal frameworks reproduce and legitimate existing power relations, highlighting the ways in which formal legal equality may obscure substantive inequalities in the digital domain.
• Digital Rights Theory: Drawing on the work of scholars such as Mireille Hildebrandt and Helen Nissenbaum, this perspective conceptualises privacy as a fundamental right that enables individual autonomy and democratic participation.
• Political Economy of the Internet: This framework, associated with scholars including Christian Fuchs and Trebor Scholz, analyses the relationship between digital platforms, capital accumulation, and labour exploitation.

At the intersection of these theoretical frameworks lies the recognition that digital governance represents a site of ongoing contestation between competing interests and values.

3. Empirical Analysis: GDPR Enforcement Patterns

An analysis of enforcement actions by Data Protection Authorities (DPAs) across EU member states reveals significant disparities in the implementation of GDPR provisions. Between May 2018 and December 2022, a total of 1,619 fines were imposed under the GDPR, with a combined value of €2.77 billion. However, the distribution of these penalties was highly uneven, with just five cases accounting for 68% of the total financial penalties imposed.

The following factors have been identified as contributing to this enforcement disparity:

1. Resource constraints: Many DPAs, particularly in smaller member states, lack the technical expertise and financial resources necessary to conduct effective investigations of complex data processing operations.
2. Jurisdictional complexity: The "one-stop-shop" mechanism, which designates a lead supervisory authority for cross-border processing activities, has been criticised for concentrating enforcement responsibilities in a small number of jurisdictions.
3. Legal complexity: The interpretation of key GDPR concepts, such as "legitimate interests" and "consent," has been the subject of significant disagreement between DPAs and national courts.

As Lilian Edwards (2020, p. 48) observes:

"The GDPR has proven to be a remarkably comprehensive and ambitious piece of legislation, but its implementation has exposed fundamental tensions between the aspiration for pan-European data protection and the reality of fragmented national enforcement capacity."

4. Platform Accountability and Self-Regulation

Social media platforms have increasingly adopted self-regulatory mechanisms as a means of managing regulatory pressure and maintaining operational flexibility. Furthermore, these mechanisms have been criticised for their lack of transparency, accountability, and democratic legitimacy. Moreover, the concentration of market power in the hands of a small number of platform operators raises significant concerns about the effectiveness of competition law as a tool for promoting pluralism and diversity in the digital public sphere.

The tension between platform self-regulation and legislative oversight is captured in the following observation by the European Data Protection Board (EDPB):

"The development of technologies such as artificial intelligence, facial recognition, and micro-targeted advertising poses significant challenges to existing data protection frameworks and may require the development of new regulatory approaches that can respond effectively to rapidly evolving technological capabilities."

In conclusion, the comprehensive evidence reviewed herein suggests that while the GDPR has made important contributions to the protection of digital rights in Europe, significant challenges remain. Future regulatory developments should focus on strengthening enforcement capacity, clarifying jurisdictional ambiguities, and developing new regulatory tools that can address the distinctive challenges posed by algorithmic decision-making and data-driven surveillance. Furthermore, a broader societal dialogue is needed to determine the appropriate balance between the imperatives of technological innovation and the protection of fundamental rights and democratic values.
